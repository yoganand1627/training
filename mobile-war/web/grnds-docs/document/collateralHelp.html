Contacting Collaterals

Social workers providing services to children and families depend on the assessment of other individuals, who maintain contact with the child and family, to determine some of the following, but not limited to:
  • Whether safety concerns exist;
  • The ability of the parent to protect his or her child;
  • The extent of the child’s vulnerabilities;
  • The individual’s relationship to the family and the potential for support; and
  • Service provision.

To gain insight on how an individual (i.e. child, caregiver, parent, etc.) is functioning, it is essential that contacts made with collaterals are deliberate and the purpose is well thought out. It is of great importance to select the most appropriate collateral who will provide information to assess the safety, permanency and/or well-being of the child and family.

It is best to ask open-ended questions, those that require more than a "yes" or "no" answer, so that a wider response to the question is gathered. Examples of information to gather include, but are not limited to the following:
  • If maltreatment exists;
  • If safety threats or impending danger exist;
  • The caregiver’s parenting practices (involvement with their children);
  • The caregiver’s discipline methods and situations in which they are used;
  • How the adult/child functions in their daily life; and
  • Behaviorally specific information that helps determine the extent of improvement.
